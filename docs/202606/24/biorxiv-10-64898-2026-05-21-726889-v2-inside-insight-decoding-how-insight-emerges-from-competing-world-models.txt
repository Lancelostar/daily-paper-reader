# Abstract 16 

When and how does insight emerge? We conceptualize insight as a sudden realization arising from 17 

restructuring a world model: an internal interpretation linking actions to outcomes. Yet these latent 18 

dynamics remain difficult to access, even with behavior and verbal report. Here we developed inside 19 

insight dynamics (IID), a machine-learning framework that estimates latent world-model dynamics 20 

from behavioral data. Using IID, we analyzed mouse behavior in indirect- and direct-rule tasks, each 21 

requiring a shift from an initial world model to a rule-consistent representation. IID inferred the 22 

“when” of insight-like shifts by estimating the timing of transitions between competing world models, 23 

and examined the “how” by comparing alternative learning processes underlying them. This analysis 24 

revealed distinct mechanisms of world-model learning: the indirect- and direct-rule tasks were better 25 

explained by gated learning and parallel learning, respectively. Thus, IID opens a route to quantifying 26 

latent insight dynamics from observable behavior alone. 27 

# Introduction 28 

In daily life, flexible problem solving often requires more than the gradual refinement of an existing 29 

strategy. In some situations, a solution emerges only after a qualitative change in how the problem 30 

itself is represented [1, 2]. Such insight-like change is often described as an “Aha!” moment, in which an 31 

individual abruptly shifts from one interpretation of a situation to another [3]. A useful historical analogy 32 

is a conceptual shift from a geocentric to a heliocentric view of planetary motion: the same observations 33 

become explainable once the underlying representational framework changes [4]. Although insight has 34 

long been studied in psychology and cognitive neuroscience, its underlying neural and computational 35 

mechanisms remain difficult to define quantitatively [2, 3]. 36 

One way to formalize insight-like change is as a restructuring of an internal “world model.” A world 37 

model is an internal representation of how the external environment is organized and how actions lead 38 

to outcomes [5, 6]. From the perspective of the Bayesian brain hypothesis, such a model can be viewed 39 

as a generative model that enables an agent to infer hidden causes of observations and predict future 40

competing world models: the agent stops interpreting the environment through one model and begins 42 

to rely on another model that better explains the observed action-outcome relationship. 43 

However, this view raises an important computational question. If multiple candidate world models 44 

exist, how are they learned and selected [6, 9]? One possibility is a gated learning mechanism, in 45 

which each world model is updated in proportion to the agent’s current reliance on that model [10]. 46 

Under this mechanism, insight-like change is not merely a sudden behavioral readout, but a process in 47 

which an emerging world model gradually becomes available for learning as it gains internal weight. An 48 

alternative possibility is a parallel learning mechanism, in which multiple candidate world models are 49 

updated simultaneously, regardless of their current contribution to behavior. In this case, an apparent 50 

moment of insight may reflect the later selection of a model that has already been learned implicitly [5]. 51 

Distinguishing between these alternatives is essential for understanding whether insight-like behavior 52 

arises from belief-dependent selective learning or from parallel latent learning followed by behavioral 53 

expression. 54 

Insight has been extensively studied in humans, often by combining behavioral performance, brain 55 

activity, and subjective reports of the moment of insight [2, 3, 11]. However, subjective reports provide 56 

only limited access to the underlying temporal dynamics, and non-invasive measurements such as fMRI 57 

and EEG, by themselves, are not sufficient to establish circuit-level mechanisms. Animal models offer 58 

the possibility of invasive neural recording, manipulation, and genetic intervention, but they lack verbal 59 

report. This makes it difficult to identify when an animal undergoes an insight-like change in internal 60 

representation. Therefore, a method is needed to infer latent insight dynamics directly from observable 61 

behavior, without relying on subjective report. 62 

We previously developed a visual discrimination-based learning task in mice to study cue-guided 63 

inhibition behavior, and showed that dopamine D2 receptor activity contributes to avoidance of a cue 64 

associated with reward omission [12]. 65 

In the present study, we reanalyzed two related touchscreen-based tasks from that study, which we 66 

refer to here as an indirect-rule task and a direct-rule task, corresponding to the original VD-Inhibit 67 

and VD-Attend tasks, respectively ( Fig. 1a ). In the indirect-rule task, one fixed cue was consistently 68 

associated with reward omission, whereas other randomly presented cues were rewarded; therefore, mice 69 

had to learn to inhibit responses to the fixed cue and choose the alternative cue. In the direct-rule task, 70 

by contrast, one fixed cue was consistently rewarded, whereas the other randomly presented cues were 71 

unrewarded; therefore, mice had to learn to choose the fixed cue. Because cue positions varied across 72 

trials in both tasks, successful performance required learning a cue-based reward rule rather than a 73 

simple left-right choice rule. Thus, the two tasks differed in task structure: the indirect-rule task 74 

required animals to infer the rewarded choice by avoiding a specific non-rewarded cue, whereas the 75 

direct-rule task involved a more direct cue-reward association. 76 

In this study, we developed inside insight dynamics (IID), a computational framework for estimating 77 

latent insight dynamics from trial-by-trial action and reward sequences. IID assumes that behavior is 78 

generated by competition between two candidate world models: a side-dependent model, in which 79 

reward is interpreted as depending on the chosen side, and a cue-dependent model, in which reward is 80 

interpreted as depending on cue identity. The method estimates both reward beliefs within each model 81 

and a time-varying insight variable representing their relative dominance. By comparing gated learning 82 

and parallel learning models, IID further tests how alternative world models are learned during insight-83 

like transitions. Applied to mouse behavior, IID revealed task-dependent mechanisms: indirect-rule 84 

task was better explained by gated learning, whereas direct-rule task was more consistent with parallel 85 

learning. These results suggest that insight-like behavioral change is not governed by a single fixed 86 

computational mechanism, but depends on task structure. More broadly, IID provides a quantitative 87 

approach for inferring hidden restructuring of internal world models from behavior alone. 88

Workflow for decoding insight dynamics from behavior 90 

We first outline the overall workflow of this study ( Fig. 1 ). We analyzed trial-by-trial behavioral 91 

data from two touchscreen-based visual discrimination tasks: an indirect-rule task and a direct-rule 92 

task, corresponding to the original VD-Inhibit and VD-Attend tasks, respectively. In both tasks, mice 93 

selected either the left or right option and received reward or no reward depending on a cue-based task 94 

rule ( Fig. 1a ). To explain these behavioral sequences, we constructed an agent decision-making model 95 

in which the agent internally maintains two competing world models of the task structure ( Fig. 1b ). 96 

These consisted of a side-dependent model, in which reward is interpreted as depending on the chosen 97 

side, and a cue-dependent model, in which reward is interpreted as depending on cue identity. In this 98 

decision-making model, the agent sequentially updates belief states within each world model and selects 99 

actions according to their relative dominance. 100 

We defined insight dynamics as the temporal change in this relative dominance, represented by 101 

a latent weighting variable wt (Fig. 1b ). A low value of wt corresponds to stronger reliance on the 102 

side-dependent model, whereas a high value of wt corresponds to stronger reliance on the cue-dependent 103 

model. This relative dominance was used to define two candidate learning mechanisms ( Fig. 1b, right ). 104 

In the gated learning formulation, belief updating within each world model is weighted by the current 105 

reliance on that model. In the parallel learning formulation, both world models are updated indepen-106 

dently of their current contribution to behavior. Thus, a transition in wt represents an insight-like 107 

restructuring from a side-based interpretation of the task to a cue-based interpretation, while compari-108 

son between the two learning formulations tests how this restructuring is acquired. We then developed 109 

inside insight dynamics (IID), an observer-side inference framework that formulates the inverse problem 110 

of this decision-making model and decodes latent recognition states and insight dynamics from the ob-111 

served action–reward sequences and task-defined cue-category mappings ( Fig. 1c and Supplementary 112 

Fig. S1 ). This workflow allowed us to ask when mice shifted between world models and whether such 113 

shifts were better explained by gated learning or parallel learning. 114 

Behavioral transitions reflect restructuring of the world model 115 

We next characterized the overt behavioral transitions observed during learning. In the indirect-rule 116 

task, mice initially showed an imbalance in left versus right choice probabilities, indicating that early 117 

behavior was biased toward spatial choice rather than cue identity ( Fig. 2a ). As training progressed, 118 

this side bias gradually disappeared, suggesting that the animals no longer relied primarily on a left– 119 

right choice strategy. Consistent with this transition, the fraction of correct choices initially remained 120 

near chance level but later increased to above 80% ( Fig. 2b ). The cumulative reward curve also showed 121 

a marked increase in slope, indicating an abrupt improvement in reward acquisition ( Fig. 2c ). A similar 122 

behavioral transition was observed in the direct-rule task. Mice again showed an initial imbalance in left 123 

versus right choice probabilities, suggesting that early choices were influenced by spatial bias ( Fig. 2d ). 124 

This bias was subsequently reduced as learning proceeded. The fraction of correct choices increased from 125 

near chance level to above 80% ( Fig. 2e ), and the cumulative reward curve showed a corresponding 126 

increase in slope ( Fig. 2f ). Thus, in both tasks, mice shifted from an early spatially biased strategy 127 

to a later cue-rule-consistent strategy. Individual behavioral trajectories for all mice are shown for the 128 

indirect- and direct-rule tasks in Supplementary Fig. S2 and Fig. S3 , respectively. 129 

These behavioral changes suggest that learning involved a change in how animals interpreted the 130 

task structure. We interpreted the early phase as behavior that may be described by stronger reliance 131 

on a side-dependent world model, whereas the later phase may be described by stronger reliance on a 132 

cue-dependent world model. We therefore used these overt behavioral transitions as a starting point for 133 

modeling the latent dynamics of world-model reliance over trials. 134

Decision-making with competing world models 135 

To account for abrupt behavioral transitions during learning, we hypothesized that the animals’ choices 136 

were generated by a dynamic interplay between competing internal world models rather than by a single 137 

fixed model. Based on this idea, we constructed an agent decision-making model in which action selection 138 

arises from the weighted integration of two candidate world models: a side-dependent model and a cue-139 

dependent model. In the side-dependent model, reward is assumed to be generated from latent states 140 

corresponding to side-dependent reward probabilities. Under this model, the agent attempts to infer the 141 

reward probabilities associated with the left and right choices. In the cue-dependent model, reward is 142 

assumed to be generated from latent states corresponding to cue-dependent reward probabilities. Under 143 

this model, the agent attempts to infer the reward probabilities associated with the specific cue and 144

Inside insight dynamics method 174 

Here, we developed a machine-learning method, termed inside insight dynamics (IID), to infer latent 175 

internal dynamics underlying insight-like behavioral transitions from observable action and reward se-176 

quences ( Fig. 1c ). IID introduces an inverse state-space model from the observer’s perspective, which 177 

we refer to as the observer-SSM, to infer the hidden internal states of the agent that give rise to observ-178 

able behavioral sequences. 179 

The observer-SSM assumes that the animal’s behavior is generated by the internal Bayesian decision-180 

making model described above, in which two competing world models, a side-dependent model and a 181 

cue-dependent model, jointly govern decision-making. These models are characterized by latent reward 182 

states, zside  

> t

and zcue  

> t

, and by an internal weighting variable wt that controls the relative influence of 183 

the two world models on behavior. Based on the observed action–reward history, the observer-SSM 184 

infers, at each trial, the hidden variables from the observer’s perspective: namely the estimated reward 185 

probabilities under each world model, the confidence associated with those estimates, and the relative 186 

reliance on the competing world models. The inference was implemented using a particle filter (see 187 

Methods). Through this framework, IID enables the observer to track latent restructuring of internal 188 

world-model dynamics during learning. 189

We next examined how latent reward beliefs evolved within each world model ( Fig. 3c ). Early in 211 

learning, updating was dominated by the side-dependent model, reflecting the animal’s stronger reliance 212 

on this model at the beginning of the task. Under the side-dependent model, the estimated reward 213 

probabilities for the left and right choices fluctuated around 0.5, consistent with the fact that reward 214 

was obtained with approximately equal probability on the two sides. Confidence in these estimates 215 

remained modest throughout this phase, indicating that the side-dependent model did not provide a 216 

highly reliable account of the task structure. 217 

Around trial 100, however, the animal began to assign non-negligible weight to the cue-dependent 218 

model, suggesting that it had started to consider the possibility that reward might depend on cue identity 219 

rather than choice side. Because learning was gated by the current reliance on each world model, this 220 

shift allowed the cue-dependent model to be updated more strongly. Accordingly, the estimated reward 221 

probabilities under the cue-dependent model became increasingly differentiated. Around trial 200, when 222 

reliance on the two models became comparable ( wt ≈ 0.5), the cue-dependent model rapidly converged 223 

on the correct task structure: the reward probability associated with the specific cue approached 0, 224 

whereas that associated with the other cues approached 1.0. At the same time, confidence in the cue-225 

dependent estimates increased sharply, indicating the formation of a reliable internal representation of 226 

the task structure. 227 

As another example of IID estimation, we examined a second representative mouse performing the 228 

indirect-rule task ( Fig. 3d, e ). In this mouse, the estimated reliance on the cue-dependent model did 229 

not exhibit a clear abrupt transition, but instead increased gradually with a temporary decrease before 230 

eventually stabilizing near the cue-dependent state ( Fig. 3d ). The corresponding belief trajectories 231 

nevertheless showed increasing separation of cue-dependent reward probabilities, together with a later 232 

increase in precision ( Fig. 3e ). These results suggest that IID can capture not only clear insight-233 

like transitions, but also more gradual and non-monotonic changes in world-model reliance, in which 234 

competition between alternative models persists before convergence. 235

Our analyses thus far have assumed a gated learning mechanism, whereby learning in each world model 237 

is weighted by the animal’s current reliance on that model. Under this view, once the animal begins to 238 

assign partial weight to a new world model, learning within that model proceeds in proportion to its 239 

internal weight. This formulation captures the idea that animals selectively update hypotheses that are 240 

currently considered relevant, rather than updating all possible interpretations of the task equally. 241

# Discussion 302 

In this study, we developed inside insight dynamics (IID), a computational framework based on the 303 

premise that an agent may have multiple competing world models and that learning depends on how 304 

these models are explored, updated, and selected. Within this framework, we operationally defined 305 

insight-like change as a transition between internal world models. We applied IID to mouse two-choice 306 

tasks in which successful performance required a shift between competing world models, and found that 307 

the resulting abrupt behavioral transitions are better understood not simply as changes in performance, 308 

but as latent restructuring of the internal world models through which animals interpret task structure. 309 

In this sense, the present work provides a quantitative approach for studying insight-like behavioral 310 

change as an internal restructuring process rather than as an externally observed shift in performance 311 

alone. 312 

A central question in this study is what computational mechanism underlies the inferred restruc-313 

turing of internal world models. In particular, the IID method allowed us to distinguish between two 314 

alternatives: a gating mechanism, in which learning within each world model depends on the animal’s 315 

current reliance on that model, and a parallel learning mechanism, in which multiple candidate world 316 

models are updated in parallel. Our results showed that the answer depended on task structure. In 317 

the indirect-rule task, behavior was better explained by the gating formulation, suggesting that a newly 318 

emerging world model became learnable only as the animal began to assign weight to it. By contrast, in 319 

the direct-rule task, the parallel learning formulation provided a better account, consistent with the idea 320 

that the more direct cue–reward structure of this task allows competing world models to be updated in 321 

parallel. Thus, the present study does not merely identify latent restructuring as a substrate of insight-322 

like behavioral change, but further shows that the computational mechanism of that restructuring is 323 

itself task dependent. 324 

To clarify what is meant by “insight” in the present study, it is important to distinguish the present 325 

framework from the subjective “Aha!” experience often discussed in human psychology [2, 3]. This 326 

distinction is especially important in animal cognition, where insight-like behavior cannot be validated 327 

through the phenomenology that defines the human ”Aha!” experience and where the relation between 328 

human insight and putative nonhuman insight remains conceptually disputed [14, 15]. Human studies 329 

of insight have often relied on subjective report to identify the moment of insight, but such reports may 330 

not fully capture the temporal structure of the underlying process [16, 17]. In particular, under a gating 331 

mechanism, a newly emerging world model becomes learnable gradually as the agent begins to assign 332

Although the present study did not directly analyze neural activity, IID is particularly important for 337 

future neuroscience studies using animal models, such as mice, rats, and monkeys, in which insight-like 338

observable behavior, but behavioral readouts alone may be insufficient for understanding what neural 340 

activity represents unless the latent internal dynamics underlying behavior are also taken into account 341 

[6, 18]. In the present case, these dynamics correspond to insight-like restructuring, including changes 342 

in the relative weighting of competing internal world models and the confidence associated with them. 343 

Relating such latent variables to neural activity may make it possible to identify neural processes that 344 

mediate or reflect the emergence of insight-like change. 345 

A key methodological feature of the present study is that it treats behavior as arising from time-346 

varying internal states rather than from fixed latent parameters [19, 20]. Computational modeling has 347 

provided powerful tools for inferring hidden cognitive variables from behavioral data, from trial-by-trial 348 

models of decision-making to Bayesian and active-inference frameworks for learning under uncertainty 349 

[8, 21–23]. Within this broader context, the distinctive aim of IID is not simply to estimate latent 350 

variables within a predefined task model, but to infer how the internal model space itself changes over 351 

time. This inverse perspective builds on our previous efforts to infer hidden computational variables from 352 

observable behavior. Earlier studies estimated relatively fixed latent quantities, such as reward-based 353 

behavioral strategies from animal behavioral time-series data [24] and participant-specific trust-related 354 

decision-making parameters from human trust-game behavior [25]. We later extended this inverse 355 

approach to time-varying internal states, including curiosity or internally weighted information seeking 356 

in two-choice behavior [13], and optimism-pessimism bias in monkey risk-taking behavior [26]. The 357 

present study shares this inverse perspective, but differs in one crucial respect: these previous studies 358 

assumed a single world model, whereas here we explicitly assume multiple competing world models and 359 

infer transitions between them. Thus, the main conceptual advance of IID is not only that it infers 360 

time-varying internal states, but that it decodes restructuring at the level of the internal model space 361 

itself. This makes IID particularly suited to studying abrupt representational change. 362 

A major limitation of the present study is that the framework assumes behavior to arise from 363 

competition between only two candidate world models. This simplification was useful for the tasks 364 

studied here and allowed the latent restructuring process to be inferred in an interpretable manner, but 365 

the true internal hypothesis space of the animal may be richer than the binary alternatives considered 366 

here [6, 27]. Related to this, the specific forms of the candidate world models were also assumed 367 

rather than exhaustively derived from the data. For example, in the indirect-rule task, we considered 368 

a side-dependent model and a cue-dependent model, but other possible internal hypotheses may also 369 

have contributed to behavior. In particular, one could imagine alternative models in which reward is 370 

associated trial by trial with the randomly presented cue, rather than with the abstract category of 371 

”specific cue versus others.” Such possibilities were not explicitly modeled here, in part to maintain 372 

interpretability and computational tractability. The present results should therefore be understood as 373 

conditional on the candidate model set considered here. 374 

In summary, this study introduces IID as a framework for inferring latent insight dynamics from 375 

behavior and shows that abrupt behavioral transitions can be understood as internal restructuring of 376 

competing world models. Our results further reveal that the computational form of this restructuring 377 

depends on task structure, with gating learning mechanisms dominating in a indirect-rule task and 378 

parallel learning mechanisms dominating in a more direct-rule task. By making latent restructuring 379 

quantitatively inferable from behavior alone, IID opens a new avenue for studying a central yet pre-380 

viously inaccessible aspect of flexible cognition. We hope that this framework will help establish a 381 

more quantitative understanding of insight-like change and, ultimately, provide a foundation for linking 382

# Methods 384 

Behavioral tasks 385 

Behavioral data analyzed in this study were derived from trial-level data underlying the previously 386 

published mouse visual discrimination study by Nishioka et al.[12]. The data were provided by the 387 

original investigators through collaboration for the purpose of the present secondary analysis. No new 388 

behavioral experiments were performed in this study. 389 

The analyzed dataset consisted of male C57BL/6J mice that performed either the VD-Inhibit task 390 

or the VD-Attend task during behavioral learning. Mice were 8-10 weeks old at the beginning of the 391 

original behavioral experiments. The present analysis included 10 mice in the VD-Inhibit condition and 392 

12 mice in the VD-Attend condition. In the dataset analyzed here, VD-Inhibit and VD-Attend were 393 

performed by separate cohorts of mice, and no animal contributed data to both tasks. The present 394 

study focused exclusively on trial-by-trial sequences of choices and reward outcomes during behavioral 395 

acquisition and did not analyze neural recordings or intervention data reported in the original study. 396 

Mice were tested in touchscreen-based two-choice visual discrimination tasks, termed VD-Attend and 397 

VD-Inhibit. The task procedures are briefly summarized here, with full experimental details reported 398 

in the original study [12]. In each trial, animals initiated the trial by nose-poking the reward magazine, 399 

after which two visual cues were presented on the touchscreen until a response was made. A touch 400 

response to one of the two response windows determined the trial outcome. Correct responses were 401 

rewarded, whereas incorrect responses resulted in a timeout penalty. Training sessions consisted of up 402 

to 60 trials or 60 min per day, and mice were trained until they reached the criterion of more than 403 

80% correct responses for two consecutive days. In the VD-Attend task, a fixed visual cue (the marble 404 

image) served as the rewarded target, whereas responses to the alternative image, randomly selected 405 

from a predefined image set, were unrewarded and punished by a timeout. In the VD-Inhibit task, by 406 

contrast, responses to a fixed visual cue (the flag image) were designated as incorrect, whereas responses 407 

to the alternative image were rewarded. Thus, the VD-Attend task involved learning a direct cue-reward 408 

association, whereas the VD-Inhibit task required animals to suppress responses to a fixed non-rewarded 409 

cue and instead select the alternative image. 410 

Behavioral data and preprocessing 411 

Behavioral data were analyzed on a trial-by-trial basis for each mouse. For each trial, we extracted the 412 

animal’s choice and the corresponding binary reward outcome. Raw image identities were not used as 413 

model inputs. Instead, cue-related information was reduced to the task-relevant category, specific cue 414 

versus other cue, according to the fixed task rule and the recorded choice–outcome relation. Because 415 

each trial contained one fixed cue and one alternative cue, this mapping allowed the chosen and unchosen 416 

options to be assigned to the corresponding cue-dependent components used in the model. Specifically, 417 

we tracked whether the chosen option corresponded to the task-relevant target or non-target category, as 418 

determined by the reward contingency of each task. Rewarded trials were coded as 1 and unrewarded 419 

trials as 0. These trial-wise action and reward sequences were used both for descriptive behavioral 420 

analyses and as inputs to the inside insight dynamics (IID) method. 421 

The analysis focused on the initial learning period of each task, from the first training session to 422 

the session in which the animal reached the learning criterion. Sessions after criterion acquisition were 423 

not included in the main IID analysis. Trials with missing action or reward records were excluded 424 

from analysis. Animals were included if trial-level choice and reward-outcome records were available 425 

throughout the learning period required for IID inference. No additional animals were excluded after 426 

applying these criteria. 427
